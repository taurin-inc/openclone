# Your Startup's 2 Million Users Are Worthless

### Here's Where the Real Money Is

**Author:** [애당초 미디움-레어](https://substack.com/@ethancho)  
**Date:** Feb 11, 2026

When Korea’s biggest department store flagship reported its 2025 numbers, one stat jumped out: 52% of its $2.3 billion in annual revenue came from VIP customers alone. Not the crowds. Not the foot traffic. Half the revenue from a handful of people who spend over $75,000 a year on luxury goods.

This wasn’t an outlier. Korea’s top three department stores — Lotte, Shinsegae, and Hyundai — all crossed 46-47% of total revenue from VIPs in 2025. Luxury goods sales grew at double the rate of overall revenue across all three chains. These companies are actually *raising* their VIP thresholds because too many customers now qualify — and the truly high-spending ones demand better service.

The same pattern shows up everywhere you look.

**Why Korea’s biggest spenders are 40+, not 24**

Golf club memberships in Korea hit an all-time high average of roughly $200,000 — in a country the size of Indiana. Thirteen courses now trade above $750,000, up from nine just four years ago. One course, Jack Nicklaus GC Korea, has held steady at $2.2 million for nine straight months. These aren’t corporate perks. They’re individuals writing personal checks.

Even in e-commerce — supposedly the domain of the young — the 40-60 demographic consistently shows higher average order values and stronger brand loyalty than younger cohorts. Musinsa, Korea’s biggest online fashion platform built on Gen Z streetwear, saw this so clearly that it launched an entirely separate platform for women in their 40s and 50s. The pet food market tells the same story: shoppers over 40 now drive the majority of premium purchases, overtaking the twentysomethings who originally built the category.

The data points all in one direction: the consumers who spend the most, churn the least, and pay premium without flinching are overwhelmingly 40+.

And yet.

**The MAU Trap: Why startups keep building for the wrong audience**

**The MAU Trap** is a term I use to describe the pattern where founders optimize for metrics that attract funding rather than metrics that generate revenue. After 20+ years in tech investing — from Google Korea to Qualcomm Ventures to Samsung’s Strategy & Innovation Center — I’ve watched this pattern repeat across every cycle.

Here’s how it works: “2 million MAU, average age 24” makes a prettier pitch deck than “50,000 users, average age 47, $200 AOV.” VCs have been trained to look for hockey-stick user growth charts. Founders optimize for what gets funded. An entire generation of startups ends up building for the audience that looks good on slides rather than the audience that actually monetizes.

The MAU Trap creates a systematic gap between *fundable metrics* and *real business*. At TheVentures, identifying and exploiting this gap is central to how we invest.

**Seoul Beauty Club: Why targeting older consumers works**

At TheVentures — a VC firm founded by the team that built and sold Viki to Rakuten — we operate under a “founders backing founders” philosophy. Our thesis: back entrepreneurs who see what others miss.

That’s why we invested in Seoul Beauty Club, a K-beauty subscription platform that deliberately targets American women aged 30 to 60.

While most K-beauty startups chase trend-obsessed twenty-somethings on TikTok, SBC founder Leo Park — a former attorney at Korea’s top law firm Kim & Chang — went after a completely different customer. As he put it: *“Beauty commerce is broken — it’s noisy, fragmented, and trend-obsessed. Our users aren’t looking for hype. They want science, story, and results.”*

The numbers during beta alone: millions of dollars in annual recurring revenue. Over 300 K-beauty brand partnerships. An NPS above 50. And $2 million in pre-seed funding raised within 100 days.

Seoul Beauty Club is a textbook example of what I call **Founder Intelligence** — the competitive advantage that emerges when a founder combines deep cultural context with global execution capability. SBC leverages Korea’s unmatched beauty innovation ecosystem to serve an underserved American consumer segment with real spending power. That kind of cross-border insight only comes from operating at the intersection of Korean and American markets — and it’s the quality we look for in every investment.

**Why Korea is the world’s toughest consumer market — and the best testing ground**

This pattern extends beyond beauty. Korea — with its hyper-competitive consumers, world-leading digital adoption rates, and accelerating demographic shifts — is increasingly the global testing ground for consumer products and services.

Korean consumers adopt new technologies 2-3 years before most global markets. They’re demanding, disloyal to brands that don’t deliver, and ruthlessly efficient at sorting signal from noise. When a product survives Korean consumers, it can survive anywhere.

For early-stage investors, this creates an asymmetric opportunity: back founders who prove product-market fit in Korea’s pressure cooker, then help them scale globally — targeting the customers who actually pay, not the ones who merely download.

**The real question every founder should ask**

Korea’s department store VIPs account for over half of all revenue. Golf memberships are trading at $2.2 million. A K-beauty startup targeting 30-60 year-olds hit millions in ARR during beta while competitors fought over Gen Z scraps.

The money is there. The customers are there. The willingness to pay premium is there.

So why are so many startups still optimizing for users who can’t — or won’t — pay?

If your growth strategy starts with “we need to capture the 18-29 demographic,” ask yourself one honest question: is that where the revenue is, or is that just where the vanity metrics are?

Build for the customer who pays. Everything else is noise.
