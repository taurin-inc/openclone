# Korea is betting big on China to compete with Silicon Valley

### This is either genius or a strategic disaster. Probably both

**Author:** [애당초 미디움-레어](https://substack.com/@ethancho)  
**Date:** Jan 13, 2026

Last week I noticed something significant that most people missed:

Korea and China just launched a **Cross-Border Deep Tech Alliance Fund** to jointly invest in early-stage technology companies.

On the surface: “Look, Korea and China are collaborating on innovation!”

Reality check: Korea is admitting it can’t win the tech race without China.

---

**Let me explain what’s actually happening here:**

**The Geopolitical Squeeze**

Korea is in an impossible position right now:

*   **U.S. wants:** Korea to be a tech ally, stop technology transfer to China, strengthen ties
*   **China wants:** Korean companies and talent to invest in/move to China
*   **Korea wants:** To maintain both relationships and stay competitive

The Korea-China Deep Tech Fund is Korea’s way of saying: “We’re betting on China too, not just the US.”

But here’s the problem...

---

**1. It signals weakness**

When a country needs to partner with a geopolitical rival to advance AI, it’s admitting:

*   We can’t build world-class AI alone
*   We can’t keep our talent from leaving
*   We need access to Chinese capital and market

This is the opposite of “sovereignty.”

**2. The IP risk is real**

Joint Korea-China ventures = technology transfer risk.

History lesson:

*   How did China’s battery tech advance so quickly? (Joint ventures with Korea)
*   How did China’s semiconductor knowledge improve? (Joint ventures)
*   How did China’s AI scale so fast? (Partnerships + talent acquisition from everywhere)

Are we about to repeat this?

**3. It’s actually smart (uncomfortably so)**

That said... this move makes economic sense:

*   Chinese capital is abundant and eager to invest
*   Chinese market is massive for tech companies
*   Chinese AI research is genuinely at the frontier right now
*   Joint ventures can create new business models

So even if it’s risky, it’s rational.

---

**The Real Issue:**

The problem isn’t the Korea-China fund itself. The problem is that **both Korea and China are rushing to secure global AI dominance** while **the U.S. is just starting to take it seriously**.

By the time the U.S. wakes up with actual policy responses, Korea and China might have already reshaped the deep-tech landscape.

**Here’s what concerns me most:**

Korea’s ecosystem is fragmented right now:

*   Some companies/VCs go all-in on China partnerships
*   Others are afraid of U.S. sanctions
*   Startups don’t know which direction to bet

This ambiguity hurts entrepreneurship. You can’t build a world-class company when your home government’s policy direction is unclear.

---

**My advice for Korean deep-tech founders:**

1.  **If you’re building in semiconductors/chips**: US partnerships are safer. Market opportunities are there.
2.  **If you’re building in AI/software**: China partnerships make sense IF you’re willing to navigate geopolitical risk. The capital is better there right now.
3.  **If you’re building in clean tech/biotech**: Neither direction is ideal yet. Be agnostic and move based on where customers are.
4.  **If you have any chance of global product-market fit**: Stop worrying about Korean government initiatives. Build for the global market, operate from anywhere.

---

**The uncomfortable truth:**

Korea’s government is desperately trying to create a “third way” between U.S. and China dominance. But in the AI/deep-tech space, there might not be a third way. You eventually have to pick a side—or get left behind.

**What’s your prediction?**

In 5 years, will Korea be:

*   A tech ally of the U.S.?
*   A tech partner of China?
*   A bridge between both?
*   Marginalized due to indecision?

I’m genuinely curious what you think, especially if you’re watching geopolitics + tech closely.
