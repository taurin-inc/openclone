# The Optimism Tax: What $18 Billion in Prediction Market Data Reveals About Who Really Wins in Venture Capital

**Author:** [애당초 미디움-레어](https://substack.com/@ethancho)  
**Date:** Feb 14, 2026

**The Worst Bet in America Isn’t in Vegas**

Slot machines on the Las Vegas Strip return about 93 cents on the dollar. Most people know this is a bad deal. Yet on Kalshi, a CFTC-regulated prediction market, thousands of traders have voluntarily accepted returns as low as 43 cents on the dollar — betting on their convictions.

A landmark analysis of 72.1 million trades covering $18.26 billion in volume, conducted by researcher Jon Becker, reveals why: markets don’t need rational actors to function. They need a mechanism for harvesting error.

And that mechanism looks exactly like venture capital.

***

**The Wealth Transfer Machine**

Prediction markets split participants into two roles. Makers place limit orders — they set a price and wait. Takers execute immediately — they want exposure now.

The data reveals a brutal asymmetry:

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!yj3g!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ffa5d5ba2-f396-42cd-9452-52a4d065dd57_503x84.png)

Takers lose at 80 out of 99 price levels. Makers profit at the same 80.

This isn’t noise. Across 72 million trades, it’s a systematic wealth transfer from the impatient to the patient.

***

**Why People Overpay for YES**

The asymmetry gets worse when you look at direction. At the extreme — contracts priced at just 1 cent — YES bets (longshot hopes) deliver -41% expected returns. NO bets (wagering against hope) deliver +23%.

That’s a 64-percentage-point gap for the same implied probability.

The explanation is what I call the **Optimism Premium**: people systematically overpay for affirmative outcomes. They want their team to win, their candidate to succeed, their token to moon. Hope is expensive. Doubt is profitable.

This pattern scales across categories:

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!sbYr!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe7415550-9e1e-489d-8f86-55ffd21c5c99_615x142.png)

Finance is nearly perfectly efficient. Entertainment is 28 times less so. The more emotional the category, the wider the gap.

Remember this. It matters for what comes next.

***

**The Maker’s Edge: A Framework for Venture Capital**

Prediction markets are zero-sum. Venture capital is not — startups create genuine new value. But the *allocation layer* — who gets access to that value creation at what price — operates on the same dynamics as any market. And at that layer, the prediction market data maps with uncomfortable precision.

I’ve spent 20 years investing across Google Korea, Qualcomm Ventures, Samsung Strategy & Innovation Center, and now as CIO of TheVentures. The single most consistent pattern I’ve observed is what I call the **Maker’s Edge**:

**Disciplined capital extracts value from emotional capital, not through better prediction, but through better structure.**

The Maker’s Edge has three components:

**1. Patient terms over fast deployment.** Makers in prediction markets don’t chase prices. They post orders and wait. In VC, this means setting valuation discipline and walking away when terms don’t fit — even when the sector is hot, even when competitors are writing checks. At TheVentures, founded by the team that built Viki and sold it to Rakuten for $200M, we’ve seen firsthand that operational founders who’ve built and exited tend to set terms rather than chase deals. That’s not a personality trait. It’s a structural advantage.

**2. Contrarian category selection.** The prediction market data shows that emotional categories have the widest efficiency gaps. In VC, the translation is direct:

High Optimism Premium (wide gap, taker-dominated):
- AI consumer apps (”this changes everything”)
- Celebrity-endorsed consumer brands
- Whatever sector retail investors are excited about

Low Optimism Premium (narrow gap, maker territory):
- B2B infrastructure
- Fintech pipes and rails
- Regulatory technology

If retail investors are excited about your sector, you are probably the taker.

**3. Structure around the NO case, not the YES case.** This is where the prediction market data becomes actionable. YES contracts at low prices systematically destroy value. NO contracts at the same prices systematically create it. The translation: don’t invest in the pitch deck. Invest in what survives when the pitch deck is wrong.

***

**How the Maker’s Edge Works in Practice**

When we evaluated a K-beauty startup, the founder’s pitch was pure YES: K-beauty is exploding globally, millennials and Gen Z will drive growth, premium segment is wide open.

Instead of buying the YES contract, we stress-tested the NO case. Gen Z has negative discretionary cash flow in Korea. K-beauty is commoditizing at the low end, compressing margins. The founder’s target demo looked exciting but couldn’t pay.

The data told a different story. Korea’s top department stores generate 52% of revenue from VIP customers aged 40 and above. The 40-60 demographic has 3-5x the spending power of Gen Z.

We structured the investment around the paying customer, not the viral demographic. Just as NO contracts at 1-cent prices returned +23% while YES contracts returned -41%, structuring around the bearish case produced a more durable thesis.

This is the same logic that guided my early investments in Toss and Dunamu — two companies now worth billions. When each had fewer than 10 people, the market consensus was NO on mobile payments and NO on crypto infrastructure in Korea. Most investors were waiting for the YES signal — traction, revenue, validation. We were makers. We set terms, evaluated structural advantage, and invested before consensus formed.

***

**Why Korea Is the Optimism Tax Laboratory**

Korea isn’t just a market I know well. It’s a near-perfect environment for studying the Optimism Tax in action — because the forces that drive it are amplified here.

Korea has 1.5 million retail crypto traders, most of them emotional, most of them losing. The country’s entertainment and content sector commands maximum emotional attachment — and the prediction market data tells us that entertainment carries the widest efficiency gap (4.79 pp) of any category. Korean startup investing is disproportionately relationship-driven. Corporate venture from chaebols often operates as forced allocation rather than patient capital. Government fund mandates create artificial deployment pressure.

All of these are taker behaviors at scale.

But this inefficiency is precisely where the Maker’s Edge generates returns. When an entire market overpays for emotional conviction, the disciplined investor faces less competition for underpriced assets. Korea’s extreme demographics — a 0.72 fertility rate creating urgent demand for AI-driven productivity solutions — mean the structural opportunities are enormous. The Optimism Tax just determines who captures them at what price.

***

**The Maturation Problem**

One caveat the data demands honesty about: the Optimism Tax wasn’t always operative. In Kalshi’s early days (2021-2023), the pattern was reversed — takers won and makers lost. The shift happened when volume exploded from $30M to $820M in a single quarter.

The VC parallel is instructive. In the early days of venture (2010-2015), spray-and-pray worked. Low competition and abundant inefficiency meant that undisciplined capital could still win. Today, with $300+ billion deployed annually into startups globally and sophisticated multi-stage funds competing at every round, taker behavior gets systematically punished.

Market maturation is a one-way door. The Optimism Tax is not a temporary anomaly. It’s the equilibrium state of any market with sufficient volume and participation. VC reached that threshold years ago. The implication: maker behavior isn’t just an edge. It’s table stakes for survival.

***

**Three Rules for Investors**

**Be a maker.** Set terms and wait. Deploy capital when others are retreating, not when they’re competing. The data shows that patience isn’t a virtue — it’s a 2.24% structural advantage per trade.

**Avoid emotional categories.** The wider the efficiency gap, the more you’re paying the Optimism Premium. If the category generates excitement in popular media, price it accordingly.

**Stress-test the NO case.** When a founder says “we’ll grow 10x,” ask: what if you only grow 2x? What if the TAM is 10% of your projection? If the investment still works under pessimistic assumptions, you’re the maker. If it requires the best case to break even, you’re the taker — and the data says you’ll pay for it.

***

**The Tax You Choose to Pay**

The Optimism Tax is not a market failure. It is the market working exactly as designed — charging a premium for hope and rewarding patience with profit. Someone always pays it. The only question is whether you’re collecting or contributing.

Seventy-two million trades and $18 billion in volume have made the answer empirical. Choose your role accordingly.
